# Creative Problem Solving

## Overview

Creative Problem Solving (CPS) is a structured approach to finding innovative solutions to challenges. Unlike routine problem solving that applies known methods, CPS deliberately seeks novel approaches when standard solutions fall short. It balances creative thinking with critical analysis, helping you generate breakthrough ideas while maintaining practical focus.

Think of it like being stuck in traffic: routine problem solving is checking your GPS for alternate routes. Creative problem solving is questioning whether you need to drive at all—could you work remotely, adjust your schedule, or relocate closer?

## Core Concept

Creative Problem Solving operates on a fundamental insight: **how you frame the problem shapes the solutions you discover**. CPS deliberately challenges initial problem statements, explores multiple framings, and generates many solutions before selecting the best approach.

The framework alternates between two thinking modes:
- **Divergent thinking**: Generating possibilities, exploring options, suspending judgment
- **Convergent thinking**: Evaluating options, making decisions, focusing direction

This rhythm of expand-then-focus repeats throughout the process, preventing both premature commitment to weak ideas and endless exploration without action.

## The CPS Process

### Stage 1: Clarify

**Divergent: Explore the Vision**
- What do you want to accomplish?
- What would success look like?
- What outcomes matter?
- Who are the stakeholders?

**Convergent: Focus on the Challenge**
- What is the core challenge?
- Where should you focus effort?
- What problem should you tackle first?

**Output**: A clear understanding of what you're trying to achieve

### Stage 2: Ideate

**Divergent: Generate Ideas**
- Brainstorm many potential solutions
- Use creativity techniques
- Build on others' ideas
- Encourage wild possibilities
- Defer judgment

**Convergent: Select Promising Ideas**
- Establish evaluation criteria
- Screen ideas for potential
- Cluster related concepts
- Choose ideas worth developing

**Output**: A set of promising solution concepts

### Stage 3: Develop

**Divergent: Explore Possibilities**
- How could you strengthen the idea?
- What variations exist?
- How might you overcome obstacles?
- What resources would you need?

**Convergent: Build a Solution**
- Refine the most promising concept
- Create an action plan
- Identify requirements and constraints
- Prepare for implementation

**Output**: A well-developed, actionable solution

### Stage 4: Implement

**Divergent: Consider Approaches**
- How might you introduce the solution?
- Who needs to be involved?
- What could help adoption?
- How will you measure success?

**Convergent: Plan and Execute**
- Define concrete next steps
- Assign responsibilities
- Set timelines
- Monitor and adjust

**Output**: A solution in action with feedback mechanisms

## Example Application

**Challenge**: Your software team consistently misses deadlines.

### Stage 1: Clarify

**Divergent exploration**:
- Projects feel rushed and stressful
- Features are cut at the last minute
- Team morale is declining
- Stakeholder trust is eroding
- Quality suffers under time pressure

**Convergent focus**:
Problem statement: "How might we create realistic project timelines that the team can consistently meet while maintaining quality and morale?"

### Stage 2: Ideate

**Divergent generation**:
- Add more buffer time to estimates
- Reduce scope of each release
- Improve estimation techniques
- Build prototypes before committing
- Parallel work streams
- Better stakeholder communication
- Cut non-essential meetings
- Pair programming to reduce bugs
- No meetings during core focus time
- Historical data analysis for estimates

**Convergent selection**:
Top 3 approaches (based on quick wins + high impact):
1. Improve estimation with historical data
2. Reduce scope per release
3. Protect core focus time from meetings

### Stage 3: Develop

**Divergent possibilities**:
For estimation improvement:
- Track actual vs. estimated time
- Analyze patterns in overruns
- Use story points consistently
- Include learning time in estimates
- Factor in interruptions and context switching
- Create estimation guidelines

**Convergent refinement**:
Action plan:
1. Start tracking time per task (2 weeks)
2. Analyze patterns in team retrospective
3. Create estimation adjustment formula based on historical data
4. Apply adjusted estimates to next sprint
5. Compare results and iterate

### Stage 4: Implement

**Divergent approaches**:
- Introduce at sprint planning
- Train team on tracking method
- Use project management tool for data
- Weekly review of estimates vs. actuals
- Share learnings in retrospectives

**Convergent execution**:
- Week 1: Set up time tracking in existing tool
- Week 2: Team tracks all tasks
- Week 3: Analyze data in retrospective
- Week 4: Apply learnings to next sprint planning
- Ongoing: Monitor and adjust formula monthly

## Key Principles

**Defer judgment**: Evaluation shuts down idea generation. Separate creation from criticism.

**Ask divergent questions**: "How might we...?" invites possibilities. "Why can't we...?" invites defenses.

**Challenge assumptions**: "We have to..." or "We can't..." statements often hide opportunities.

**Seek quantity**: More ideas increase the odds of finding a great one. Aim for volume before quality.

**Build on others**: "Yes, and..." creates momentum. "Yes, but..." stops it.

**Embrace wild ideas**: Impractical ideas can spark practical ones. Don't dismiss them too quickly.

## Common Pitfalls

**Jumping to solutions**: Starting with "here's what we should do" skips problem exploration. You might solve the wrong problem elegantly.

**Stopping at the first good idea**: The first idea feels like relief, but it's rarely the best idea. Generate more before choosing.

**Mixing divergence and convergence**: Evaluating ideas while generating them kills creativity. Your brain can't do both modes simultaneously.

**Ignoring constraints too long**: Pure ideation without reality checks wastes time on impossible solutions. Balance creativity with feasibility.

**Analysis paralysis**: Overanalyzing options delays action. Set decision deadlines and commit.

**Skipping implementation planning**: Great ideas that aren't executed remain ideas. Plan for action.

## Techniques and Tools

**For clarification**:
- 5 Whys (dig deeper into root causes)
- Stakeholder mapping
- Vision statements
- Goal hierarchies

**For ideation**:
- Brainstorming
- SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse)
- Mind mapping
- Random word association
- Six Thinking Hats

**For development**:
- Pros/cons/implications analysis
- Feasibility assessment
- Prototyping
- Pilot testing
- Risk analysis

**For implementation**:
- Action planning
- Stakeholder communication plans
- Success metrics
- Feedback loops

## When to Use CPS

Use Creative Problem Solving when:
- Standard solutions haven't worked
- The problem is complex or ambiguous
- Innovation is needed
- Stakeholders have conflicting views
- You feel stuck or overwhelmed
- The cost of failure is low enough to experiment

Use simpler approaches when:
- The problem is routine and well-understood
- Time is extremely limited
- A proven solution exists
- The context is highly constrained
- Stakeholders resist change

## Developing CPS Skills

**Practice divergent thinking**:
- Set quantity goals (30 ideas in 15 minutes)
- Use random prompts to break patterns
- Study unrelated fields for inspiration
- Suspend judgment consciously

**Practice convergent thinking**:
- Define criteria before evaluating
- Use structured decision matrices
- Practice saying "no" to good options
- Set decision deadlines

**Strengthen problem framing**:
- Rewrite problems multiple ways
- Use "How might we..." questions
- Challenge your first problem statement
- Seek diverse perspectives on the problem
